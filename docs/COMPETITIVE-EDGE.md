# What the Competition Does That We Don’t — And How to Be Better

> Drop in the repo at `docs/COMPETITIVE-EDGE.md`. Based on 2026 research into the
> leading kids’ chore/habit apps (PointUp, OurHome, S’moresUp, Greenlight,
> FamilyMeritTracker, Nori, Cozi Chores, Homey, BusyKid, Questmo, ChoreQuest).
> A map of gaps + differentiators, NOT a build list. Pick what fits the app’s
> soul; ignore the rest.

## First: what we ALREADY do as well as or better than the leaders

Don’t lose sight of this — a lot of the “winning features” are things you have:

- **Deep gamification** (stars economy, the board game, treasure) — PointUp wins
  on “RPG quests, levels, badges”; your board game + stars is the same league,
  arguably more original than a generic XP bar.
- **Photo proof** — nearly every top app lists this as a key trust feature; you
  have it (chore camera proof, the camera-vs-library logic).
- **Streaks** — your 312-day streak is exactly the momentum mechanic these apps
  chase.
- **Parent approval flow** — core trust feature; you have it, well-built.
- **Multi-profile / per-kid** — standard; you have it.
- **Memories / photo gallery / slideshows** — most apps DON’T have this; it’s a
  real differentiator you already shipped.
- **Custom rewards parents define** — you have it.
- **Progress insights** — you have it.
  So the foundation is strong. The gaps below are additions, not catch-up on the core.

## What they do that we DON’T (ranked by fit + impact)

### 1. A visual “growth” metaphor kids check daily (HIGH fit, high impact)

FamilyMeritTracker’s single most-praised feature: **a growing oak tree that
evolves through 8 stages as points accumulate, with seasonal changes and 30+
collectible creatures.** Quote: “Kids check it daily. No other app has anything
like it.” This is pure delight — a living thing that grows with the kid’s
effort, separate from the spend-it economy.

- **Why it fits you:** you already have a board game + themes (Volcano, Dino,
  Enchanted Forest). A persistent “grows over time” visual (a tree, a world, a
  creature collection) that reflects cumulative progress would be a natural,
  beloved addition — and it gives long-term momentum the daily board doesn’t.
- **The insight:** spendable stars motivate short-term; a thing that only GROWS
  (never spent) motivates long-term. Two different psychological hooks. The
  leaders use both.

### 2. Low-friction input: voice / photo / scan (HIGH fit, medium build)

The newest wave (Nori, Jam, Goldee) wins entirely on **reducing typing**:

- Voice: “Add vacuum living room to Jake’s list.”
- Snap a handwritten chore chart → it becomes tasks.
- Forward an email → it becomes an event/task.
- **Why it matters for you:** you’ve felt this pain — re-inputting books/songs is
  tedious. Voice or quick-add would help parents AND make adding Reznor’s stuff
  fast. The book/song custom-cover-by-photo you built is already in this spirit.
- Start small: a faster quick-add, maybe voice-to-text for adding a task/book.
  Full AI email-parsing is a “someday,” not now.

### 3. ADHD / neurodiverse-friendly modes (MEDIUM fit, real differentiator)

PointUp’s standout: “the ONLY ADHD-friendly features on the market — Focus Mode,
Calm Mode, sensory settings, visual timers.” Others have a **visual task
sequencer: one task at a time in a journey-path view, preventing overwhelm.**

- **Why it could fit:** your Top 8 can feel like “a lot for a kid in a day” (your
  words). A “one task at a time / focus mode” view — show just the current thing,
  not the whole overwhelming list — directly addresses that. Even if Reznor isn’t
  neurodiverse, “one thing at a time” reduces overwhelm for any young kid.
- Low-effort version: a kid-view toggle that shows ONE task at a time with a
  visual timer, instead of the full board, for days when the full list overwhelms.

### 4. Financial literacy / real allowance (LOW-MEDIUM fit)

Greenlight, BusyKid, Rooster Money tie chores to **real money / a debit card /
saving + investing.** This is a whole category (banking-style apps).

- **Why it’s lower fit:** your stars→toys economy already teaches earn/save/spend
  in a kid-appropriate way without real banking. Going to real money is a big
  regulatory/complexity leap. Probably NOT your lane — but a “savings goal”
  visual (save stars toward a big reward, like the Universal Studios @ 500 you
  have) is the kid-appropriate version, and you already do it.

### 5. Kiosk / shared family display mode (MEDIUM fit, easy-ish)

Several apps push a **“mount an old tablet in the kitchen in Kiosk Mode”** /
family-display setup — the board always visible, kids check it walking by, no
nagging. “It removes the memory burden. The chore is always visible.”

- **Why it fits:** your board game is *beautiful* — it would shine as an
  always-on kitchen display. A “display/kiosk mode” (big, glanceable, auto-
  refreshing, maybe rotating between board + calendar + today’s plan) turns the
  app into the literal family command center on a wall/tablet. Strong fit with
  the “command center” name direction.

### 6. Team/sibling quests & collaboration (LOW fit now — single kid)

Apps offer “team quests for siblings,” collaborative tasks. Less relevant with
one kid, but worth noting if the family grows.

### 7. Late penalties / due times (LOW fit — philosophy mismatch)

Some apps dock points for late/missed chores. Your philosophy (habit-forming,
don’t make a partial day feel like failure, the meltdown-fix) deliberately
avoids punishment. Skip — it conflicts with your warmth.

## The HONEST competitive truth

The chore-app space is CROWDED and most apps are samey (points + rewards + a
chart). What actually differentiates the winners:

1. **A delight hook kids check daily** (the merit tree) — emotional, not just
   functional.
1. **Low-friction input** (voice/photo) — respecting parents’ time.
1. **Reducing overwhelm** (one-task-at-a-time / focus mode).
1. **Being the family hub, not just a chore chart** (calendar + meals + lists in
   one place — the command-center vision).

You already out-build most of them on gamification depth, memories, and the
board game. Your gaps are: (a) a long-term GROWTH visual, (b) faster input,
(c) an overwhelm-reducing kid view, and (d) the household-coordination layer
(calendar/meals/shopping — see FAMILY-APP-RESEARCH.md).

## How to be BETTER (not just match) — the strategic edge

- **Lean into what they can’t easily copy:** your app is deeply personal and
  custom-built for YOUR family’s real activities (drums, Spanish, taekwondo,
  swim with Mr. Voyce). Generic apps can’t match that specificity. The board
  game, the real activity schedule, the per-day Top 8 — that’s depth no
  off-the-shelf app offers.
- **Combine the two halves nobody combines:** kid-development/gamification depth
  (your strength) + family household coordination (their calendar/meals/lists).
  The research is clear almost nobody does both well. That’s the open lane.
- **Keep the warmth:** no punishment, completable boards, honest data, “life
  happened” streak freezes. The leaders are functional; you can be functional
  AND kind. That’s a real edge for a family app.

## Suggested “make it better” priorities (when staging exists + energy)

1. **A long-term growth visual** (a tree/world/collection that grows with
   cumulative progress, never spent) — the daily-check delight hook. High impact.
1. **Faster input** (quick-add, voice-to-text for tasks/books/songs) — respects
   your time, fixes the re-input pain.
1. **Focus mode / one-task-at-a-time kid view** — reduces overwhelm on big days.
1. **Display/kiosk mode** — the board as an always-on kitchen command center.
1. The household layer (shopping/meals/calendar — separate docs).

## Build discipline (non-negotiable)

Recon → branch → preview → test → merge, on STAGING once it exists. Most of
these reuse existing patterns (the growth visual derives from existing star/
completion data; focus mode is a new view of existing tasks) — the SAFE
category, no risky role/RLS changes. Confirm in recon. One at a time.

## North star

Don’t try to out-feature 20 chore apps. Be the ONE app that’s (a) as fun and
gamified as the best, (b) as warm and honest as none of them, (c) personalized
to a real family’s real life in a way generic apps can’t touch, and (d) slowly
grows into the whole-family command center that combines the kid side and the
household side nobody else unites.
