START HEREHow to run this

Five rules for the grown-ups

  • Follow his energy, not the clock. A great morning sprint is 30–45 minutes. When his interest fades, you're done — stop on a high note.
  • No tests, no grades, no worksheets. The whole edge here is that it's the opposite of school. If it starts to feel like a chore, change it.
  • Skip and swap freely. Filming days, Reznor commitments, lazy beach days — move things around. Missing a day costs nothing.
  • His four activities ARE the curriculum. Drums, hip hop, swim, taekwondo = the movement foundation everything else sits on. Don't cut them for "school."
  • Protect boredom and free play. Don't fill every gap. Unstructured time is where the inventing actually happens.

THE 5 THREADSWhat you're weaving

Make & Build

The spine of the summer

One big project he chews on for 7 weeks: invent a board game, then rebuild it on a screen. Hits art, writing, math & coding at once.

Lego Math

Singapore method

Math you can hold: real objects first, then pictures, then numbers. "Show me with bricks" before any symbol. No worksheets.

Coding

Estonia method

Start screen-free — the Sandwich Robot, Lego-by-instruction, arrow mazes. Then graduate to ScratchJr on the iPad.

Reading EN + ES

Keep both ladders climbing

He already flies here — so go deeper & wider, not faster. One stretch book always going, and Spanish kept just as alive as English.

Move & Play

Finland method

The base layer. Outdoors daily, movement woven through the day, and a nightly read-aloud together. This is non-negotiable, not optional.

THE SKELETONA week that still feels like summer

Morning sprint
30–45 min · ~4 days. Pick ONE thread for the day (rotate). Short, fresh, done before he tires.
Build time
2–3 sessions/week. The game project. Counts as art + math + writing + engineering all together.
Every day
His activities + free play + outdoors. Drums / hip hop / swim / taekwondo and a big block of unstructured play.
Bedtime
Read-aloud together. Keep the Super Sons omnibus going + the week's stretch book. A few nights a week, read in Spanish.

THE MISSION7 weeks, one game, from idea to "I made this"

WEEK 1Inventors' WorkshopLow pressure. Just dream and make.
Make & Build

Dream up the game. Pick the theme (symbiotes? Super Sons? his own heroes). Sketch & paint the board and a few cards.

Lego Math

Part-whole with bricks: "You have 12, give me 5 — show me what's left, then say the number."

Coding

The Sandwich Robot. He gives you exact steps to make a sandwich; you follow them too literally. That's debugging.

Reading

Pick the summer stretch book. After each book, he draws one comic panel of his favorite moment.

WEEK 2The RulebookTurn the idea into a real game.
Make & Build

Write the rules together (you scribe, he decides). Choose the playing pieces — Lego minifigs make great tokens.

Lego Math

Dice + counting spaces. Roll 20 times, tally the results: "Which number showed up most?"

Coding

Arrow mazes on paper. He "programs" a path (up, up, right, right) for you to walk. Intro to sequences & loops.

Reading

Spanish anchor. Read in Spanish (keep Tipos Malos going) and draw a panel in Spanish.

WEEK 3Playtest & FixThe most important week. Version 1 is never the final version.
Make & Build

Play the game as a family. Find what's broken or boring. Change it together — make Version 2.

Lego Math

Scoring with bricks: stack each player's points. "Who has more? By how many?" (That's a bar model.)

Coding

Find the Bug. Give him a silly broken instruction set ("brush teeth with shoe") — he spots & fixes the error.

Reading

Read-aloud + pause to predict: "What do you think happens next, and why?" (Comprehension, not a quiz.)

WEEK 4Hello, ScreenTake the paper game digital.
Make & Build

Open ScratchJr FREE · iPad/Android and start rebuilding the game. First goal: make a character move.

Lego Math

"Groups of" — build 3 piles of 4 bricks, count the total. The quiet beginning of multiplication.

Coding

ScratchJr basics: move a sprite, make it react to a tap. The unplugged ideas now run on a screen.

Reading

He writes & draws his own one-page comic (Cat Kid Comic Club is the perfect model — it teaches kids to make their own).

WEEK 5Make It MoveAdd interaction. Make it feel like a real game.
Make & Build

In ScratchJr: tap to make something happen, then add a second character that responds. A tiny living world.

Lego Math

Measure & build to target: "Make a tower exactly 10 bricks. Now one that's 3 taller."

Coding

Events + repeat (loops). If he's flying, peek at full Scratch in a browser together — his reading is ready for it.

Reading

Label his game or comic in both languages — every character or button gets an EN and an ES name.

WEEK 6Director's ChairHis pick. This is the Finland move — let curiosity drive.
Make & Build

He chooses where to go deeper: more art, more code, a giant Lego build with a story, or a stop-motion movie.

Lego Math

Real-world math he picks: cooking (fractions you can taste) or a pretend store with coins and change.

Coding

Finish one small, fully playable thing in ScratchJr or Scratch — start to end, his own idea.

Reading

Flip it: he reads to you. Reading aloud builds fluency and confidence. Cheer, don't correct.

WEEK 7Showcase & ShipThe payoff. He holds the thing he dreamed up.
Make & Build

Finalize both versions. Family game night using HIS game — he teaches everyone the rules (hello, confidence).

Lego Math

He's the official scorekeeper for game night — tallying, comparing, adding points for real stakes.

Coding

He demos the ScratchJr version. Then ask the magic question: "What would Version 3 be?" Keep the loop open.

Reading

Finish the stretch book and let him pick the next one — the first rung of the school-year ladder.
